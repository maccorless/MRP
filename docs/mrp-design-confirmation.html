Executive Summary

Today, press accreditation for the Olympic Games is managed through Excel spreadsheets emailed between the IOC, 206 National Olympic Committees, and the Organising Committee. When a media organization disputes a decision, the IOC cannot see what happened. When a NOC submits their list, there is no check for duplicates. When quota allocations change, everyone re-sends the same spreadsheet. For LA 2028, the IOC has committed to replacing this process with a dedicated portal — and has already communicated this commitment to NOCs worldwide.

The Media Registration Portal (MRP) is that replacement. It manages the Expression of Interest (EoI) process for press accreditation: the formal first step in which media organizations register their interest in covering the Games. It connects media organizations submitting applications, National Olympic Committees reviewing and approving applications from their territory, and IOC Media Operations with global oversight across all 206 NOCs.

Once an organization is approved, their record is passed to the Common Codes accreditation system, where individual credentialing is managed separately. The portal does not replace individual accreditation — it replaces the organization-level intake process that precedes it.

This document presents the current design for your review. Development is ready to begin and is proceeding in parallel. Several business questions are highlighted throughout where your guidance will shape the final system. We have made working assumptions in each area so that build can continue, and we are prepared to adjust based on your feedback.

Why We Are Building This

The current process creates three distinct problems that this portal is designed to solve.

1
IOC has no visibility into what NOCs do
The IOC allocates quotas to 206 NOCs and receives final approved lists — but has no window into the review process itself. When a media organization disputes a decision, the IOC cannot reconstruct what happened or why. This is a transparency and reputational risk for every Games edition.
2
No check for duplicate organizations across NOCs
A wire service such as Reuters or Associated Press legitimately operates in multiple countries. A fraudulent applicant may submit under multiple NOCs to increase their chances of approval. Today there is no way to distinguish between the two. The portal flags these cases automatically so the IOC and NOCs can make an informed decision.
3
Every change to a quota means re-sending an Excel file
When the IOC revises a quota allocation, a new version of the Excel template must be sent to the relevant NOC, who re-fills and re-sends it. Multiple file versions, no audit trail, and no easy way to confirm the right version reached the right person. The portal handles this in real time — quota changes appear immediately on the NOC's dashboard.
Design Note
This portal is designed to last beyond LA 2028
The system is being built so that an organization approved for LA 2028 does not need to re-apply from scratch for Brisbane 2032. Their record carries forward. The IOC builds a permanent, authoritative directory of accredited media organizations across Games editions — something that does not exist today.

How This Fits Into the Existing System

The portal handles the organization-level intake process — who is eligible to apply for press credentials. Individual journalist accreditation (the credentials issued to named people) remains in the LA28 accreditation system, as it has been for prior Games.

🌎

Decision Needed
Can a NOC have more than one administrator?
The current design supports multiple administrators per NOC — for example, a head press attaché and a deputy who can both log in and review applications. We can restrict this to one person per NOC if you prefer. Please advise whether multi-person NOC teams are needed for launch, or whether we should start with one login per NOC and expand later.

How Organizations Enter the System

Not every media organization will discover the portal on their own — many are known organizations who covered prior Games and expect to be contacted directly. The portal needs to handle both paths: organizations that are invited, and organizations that are new or unknown.

Provisional Decision
Known organizations are invited; new organizations self-register
Organizations that have participated in previous Games — already known to the IOC and listed in Common Codes — will receive a direct invitation by email with a secure link to begin their application. They do not need to search for the portal or discover it themselves.

Organizations that are applying for the first time, or are not yet in Common Codes, will self-register by entering their email address. This initiates a verification step to confirm the address before they can proceed.

Both paths lead to the same application form. The difference is in how they arrive.
Provisional Decision
Applicants return to the portal using the same secure link (magic link) — no password required
There is no password to remember. When an applicant needs to return to check on their application or resubmit a returned form, they enter their email address again and receive a new secure link. Clicking the link logs them in directly.

This is intentional: the verification step at the start of the application is the same authentication mechanism used for all future visits. It is simpler for applicants — especially those who may return only once or twice over several months — and eliminates the risk of forgotten passwords. Please confirm this approach is appropriate, or advise if you would prefer a more traditional account-based mechanism.
Screen 0 of 7 — Invited Organization Landing (Known Orgs Only)
When a known organization clicks their invitation link, they land here — not the email verification screen. Their IOC record is pre-filled and shown for confirmation. Two states are shown: link active (left tab) and link expired (right tab). This screen does not exist in the public-facing flow — it is only reached via an invitation link sent by IOC or the relevant NOC.
Decision Needed — Data Protection & Verification
How do we verify that a person registering is entitled to represent an organization?
This is the most important design question we have not yet resolved, and it has significant data protection implications.

If anyone can register an email address and claim to represent Associated Press or Reuters, the self-serve model is open to abuse. A random person could "claim" a well-known organization's name and potentially access or interfere with that organization's application. This is sometimes called "squatting."

The verification options we are considering are:

  • Email domain matching — we infer that a person using an @reuters.com email address is legitimately from Reuters. Simple, but it doesn't work for freelancers or organizations that use generic email providers.
  • NOC/IF confirmation — the NOC (or IF, where applicable) confirms that the person who submitted is authorized to represent that organization. This makes the NOC review step itself the verification gate. It is more robust but places additional responsibility on NOC administrators.
  • Invitation-only for known organizations — if an organization is already in Common Codes, they only enter through an invitation. This prevents squatting for known organizations entirely. Unknown organizations go through NOC review, which acts as the verification step.
We would also like to discuss the case of multiple contacts per organization. If an organization wants two people to submit on their behalf, how do we verify that the second person is genuinely from the same organization — and that sharing application data between the two is appropriate under data protection rules?

We recommend walking through these scenarios with IOC OIS before finalizing the design. The stakes are high: we cannot let the portal be used to impersonate legitimate media organizations, but we also cannot make the process so complex that genuine applicants are blocked.

The Application Journey

Step 1 — Email Verification

For organizations that are self-registering (not yet known to the IOC), the journey begins by entering their contact email address. The portal sends a secure verification link to confirm the address is valid before allowing them to proceed. Organizations that have been invited directly skip this step — their link begins at the application form. In both cases, the same secure link approach is used for all future portal visits.

Screen 1 of 7 — Email Verification
The applicant enters their email address to receive a secure one-time access link. Invited organizations skip this screen entirely and begin directly at the application form.
Provisional Decision
Verification links expire after 24 hours
We have designed the verification link to expire after 24 hours. An applicant who misses the window can simply re-enter their email to receive a new link — nothing is lost. Please confirm this window is appropriate, or advise if a longer period (for example 72 hours) would better accommodate applicants in different time zones or with limited internet access.

Step 2 — Expression of Interest Form

Once their email is verified, the applicant completes the Expression of Interest form. The form captures the organization's details, the primary contact, and the type of accreditation being sought. The process is designed to be completed in a single session — there is no save-and-resume, as the form is short enough to complete in under ten minutes.

Screen 2 of 7 — Expression of Interest Form
The application form. The contact email is pre-filled and locked from the verification step. The applicant selects their accreditation category.
Decision Needed — This Is the Most Time-Sensitive Question
What is the final list of fields on the application form?
The fields shown above are based on the Milan Cortina 2026 (MICO26) process as a starting point. IOC OIS will provide the definitive field list for LA 2028. We are ready to update the form the moment that list is confirmed. This is the one question where earlier feedback has the most impact — the form fields determine how we design the database, and changes after that point are more costly.

Are any fields above incorrect, missing, or unnecessary for the EoI stage?
Provisional Decision
Accreditation categories: Press, Photographer, ENR
We have included three accreditation categories based on the MICO26 form: Press (written media), Photographer, and ENR (Non-Rights Broadcaster). The full ENR workflow — which involves a separate undertaking agreement — is planned for a later release. Please confirm whether these three categories are correct for the EoI stage, or whether the list should be expanded or simplified.

Step 3 — Submission Confirmation

After submitting, the applicant receives a confirmation screen showing a unique reference number, a summary of what they submitted, and a plain-language explanation of what happens next — including an expected review timeline and how they will be contacted by their NOC.

Screen 3 of 7 — Submission Confirmation
The applicant receives a reference number and a plain-language explanation of next steps, including who reviews their application and how they will be notified.
Decision Needed
What review timeline should we communicate to applicants?
The confirmation screen currently says "typically 2–4 weeks." We want to set accurate expectations with applicants. What is the realistic NOC review window at this stage? Should this vary by territory, or is a single global estimate appropriate?

NOC Review — Managing Your Territory

Each National Olympic Committee has access to a dashboard showing all applications received from media organizations in their territory. The NOC administrator can search, filter, and sort applications by status, then open each one in detail before making a decision. They see only their own territory — never another NOC's applications.

Screen 4 of 7 — NOC Administrator Dashboard
The NOC dashboard. Applications are filterable by status (Pending, Flagged, Approved). The "Possible duplicate" flag is automatically raised when the system detects an organization that may already be registered in another territory.

Application Review Screen

When a NOC administrator opens an application, they see the organization's full details alongside any flags raised by the system. The review screen also shows the organization's cross-Games history — if they were accredited for Paris 2024 or Tokyo 2020, that is shown — giving the reviewer useful context before making a decision.

Screen 5 of 7 — Application Detail & Review
The review screen for a single application. The NOC administrator can approve, return the application for corrections (with a written reason sent to the applicant), or reject it. Every action is recorded in the permanent audit trail.
Design Note
Every action leaves a permanent record
Every decision made in the portal — approvals, rejections, returns, duplicate resolutions — is recorded with a timestamp and the name of the person who made it. This audit trail cannot be edited or deleted. It is the record the IOC can consult if a media organization disputes a decision at any point.
Decision Needed
Who has the authority to resolve cross-territory duplicate flags?
When the system detects that the same organization appears to have applied through multiple NOC territories (for example, Associated Press in the USA, France, and Japan), a "possible duplicate" flag appears on each relevant NOC's dashboard.

The flag is visible in the current wireframes. The resolution workflow is intentionally not yet designed — we do not have enough process knowledge to build it. Before we can design the resolution UI, we need to know who acts on it:

  • Should NOC administrators be empowered to resolve flags independently — marking an organization as "legitimate multi-territory" or "confirmed duplicate"?
  • Or should cross-territory duplicates always be escalated to IOC for final determination?
This is a question of policy, not technology. Both are buildable. What changes is whether resolution is a local NOC action (with IOC visibility) or a formal IOC workflow. The answer also determines the lifecycle states the flag needs to support: raised, under review, resolved, overridden.
Provisional Decision
Returning an application notifies the applicant by email
When a NOC administrator returns an application for corrections, the applicant automatically receives an email with the reviewer's written reason and a link to resubmit. We have assumed this is the correct behavior. Please confirm, and advise whether the NOC administrator should also receive a notification when the applicant resubmits.

Applicant Resubmission Flow

When a NOC returns an application, the applicant receives an email with the reviewer's written reason and a link to correct and resubmit. On returning to the portal, the applicant sees the reviewer's note prominently displayed, the original form pre-filled, and any changed fields highlighted in amber. They can make corrections and resubmit — up to 3 times per application.

Applicant Screen — Correct and Resubmit (Returned Application)
The applicant sees the reviewer's note in a prominent amber banner at the top of the screen. All fields are pre-filled from the original submission. Fields the applicant has changed since the original submission are highlighted in amber with a note indicating the update. The "Resubmit" button is amber (matching the returned state) to visually distinguish this action from a first submission. A note at the bottom shows the resubmission count against the limit (3 max).
Provisional Decision
Returned applications expire after 30 days without resubmission
If an applicant does not resubmit a returned application within 30 days, the application moves to an "expired" state and is removed from the active queue. This prevents incomplete applications from accumulating indefinitely. Please advise if a different window is appropriate, or if expiry should not apply at this stage of the process.

Quota Allocation — Press by Number

After the EoI review phase closes, the NOC moves into the Press by Number phase: formally allocating their IOC-assigned quota of credentials among the approved organizations in their territory. This is the step that currently happens via Excel — the portal replaces it with a dedicated assignment screen.

Screen 7 of 7 — Press by Number: Quota Assignment (default view)
The Press by Number assignment screen. The NOC's total IOC-assigned quota and running allocation totals appear in the stat cards and progress bar. Each approved organization shows prior Games allocations as context. Rows highlighted in blue have been changed from their prior-Games value. The screen auto-saves drafts; submission to IOC locks the allocations. The "History" icon on each row opens a popover showing the organization's full Games allocation history.
Screen 7 of 7 — Press by Number: Reserve Quota popup (triggered from quota bar)
The reserve quota popup appears when the NOC clicks the amber "reserved" element — either the stat card, the "10 reserved" figure in the progress bar header, or the amber legend item. The popup lets the NOC adjust how many slots to hold back from the allocation pool for late changes. It is intentionally low-prominence: most NOCs will never need to touch this, and it should not distract from the core allocation task.
Design Note
Quota totals also appear on the main NOC dashboard
The NOC's IOC-assigned quota, how much has been allocated, and how much remains are shown as a stat card on the main Applications dashboard — not only on the Press by Number screen. This gives the NOC a constant reminder of their quota headroom as they work through EoI reviews. The stat card links directly to the assignment screen.
Decision Needed
Does the NOC need a "reserved" quantity held back from assignment?
In the current Excel process, some NOCs informally hold back a portion of their quota to accommodate late applications or last-minute changes from the IOC. Should the portal formalize this as a "reserve" — a number of slots the NOC can set aside from their total that do not appear in the initial allocation pool?

  • If yes: what is the reserve — a fixed number, a percentage, or NOC-defined?
  • Does the IOC need to approve or see the reserve, or is it purely an internal NOC tool?
  • At what point does the reserve need to be committed (i.e., when does the NOC have to use it or lose it)?
Decision Needed
Does the IOC need to approve NOC quota allocations, or just have visibility?
When a NOC assigns their quota among approved organizations, does the IOC play an approval role — or is the NOC's allocation decision final, with the IOC simply able to see what was decided?

The current Excel process does not include an IOC approval step for individual allocations. If an IOC sign-off is required for v1.0, this changes the workflow and needs to be confirmed before we design the assignment screen.

IOC Oversight — Global View

IOC Media Operations has access to a global dashboard that shows activity across all 206 National Olympic Committees in one place. The IOC view is organized by NOC — allowing the IOC team to quickly see which territories are active, which are lagging, and where the system has flagged something unusual for their attention.

Screen 6 of 7 — IOC Global Dashboard
The IOC dashboard. Each NOC appears as a card showing an at-a-glance breakdown of application status. The amber banner at the top surfaces situations that may need IOC attention — for example, an unusually high volume of applications from a single territory, or the same organization applying through multiple NOCs.
Design Note
The IOC dashboard gives visibility without creating bottlenecks
The IOC's role in the portal is oversight, not approval. NOC administrators are responsible for reviewing and approving their territory's applications. The IOC dashboard is designed so that the IOC can see everything, intervene when something looks wrong, but does not become a bottleneck in the day-to-day review process. The only actions the IOC takes directly are: resolving cross-territory duplicate flags, and exporting the final approved organization list to Common Codes.
Provisional Decision
Anomaly alert: more than 80 applications from a single NOC
The system flags an unusual volume alert when a single NOC territory receives more than 80 applications. This threshold is a starting point — we would welcome your guidance on what constitutes an unusual volume. Should this vary by territory? The USA, Japan, and Germany will naturally attract more applications than smaller NOCs.
Decision Needed
How does IOC respond when an anomaly is detected?
Currently, anomalies appear on the IOC dashboard as a notification banner. No further escalation is automated — the IOC must log into the portal to see them.

We are considering also sending a daily email summary to IOC Media Operations listing any open anomalies. However, the response process — what IOC does once an anomaly is identified — is not yet defined.

  • Is a dashboard notification sufficient, or would a daily email alert be helpful?
  • Who at IOC is responsible for reviewing anomaly notifications?
  • What does IOC do to resolve an anomaly — is it an internal conversation with the relevant NOC, or is there a formal process?

Organization Directory

The Organization Directory is the cross-Games registry of all media organizations that have ever applied through MRP — whether for LA 2028, prior Games, or both. Unlike the NOC dashboard (which shows applications for the current Games only), the directory spans all editions. It is the IOC's evolving picture of the global accredited media landscape.

Each organization's entry shows their current LA 2028 application status, their Common Codes coding status, and a color-coded badge history of which Games they have previously been approved for. New organizations — those not yet in Common Codes — are flagged explicitly so IOC can trigger the coding workflow before the record is used downstream.

Admin Screen — Organization Directory
The Organization Directory. Each row shows an organization's type, country, Games history badges, Common Codes status, and current LA 2028 application status. Filters include country, org type, Games edition, and status. The "New" filter surfaces organizations applying for the first time who are not yet in Common Codes. Multi-territory organizations (like AP) appear as separate rows per territory — consistent with the flat org identity model.
Design Note
The directory is the IOC's long-term asset, not just an application list
Application lists are ephemeral — they represent one Games cycle. The Organization Directory is the permanent record. An organization approved for Tokyo 2020 and Paris 2024 that applies for LA 2028 carries their full history with them. When the portal is used for Brisbane 2032, the IOC does not start from scratch — they start from 1,800+ known organizations, with their track record already on file. This is what makes MRP a platform, not a one-off tool.

Audit Trail

Every action taken in the portal — every approval, return, rejection, login, export, and duplicate flag — is written to a permanent, immutable audit log. No administrator, including IOC super-admins, can edit or delete audit entries. The audit trail is the record the IOC can present if a media organization disputes a decision at any point.

Admin Screen — Audit Trail
The audit trail. Each entry shows a timestamp, the actor (named user or system), a color-coded action badge, the organization affected, and a detail field with the full reason or note. The trail is filterable by date range, action type, actor, and organization. Every action type has a distinct badge color: green for approvals, amber for returns, red for rejections, blue for submissions, purple for exports. The immutability note in the page header makes the no-edit policy explicit to all administrators.
Design Note
Immutability is a feature, not a constraint
The audit trail is append-only. Every entry is written once and locked. This is not a limitation of the implementation — it is a deliberate design choice. When a media organization asks "why was our application rejected in 2028?", the IOC needs to be able to show them exactly what happened, in sequence, with no possibility that the record has been altered. The trail is the IOC's legal protection and the applicant's right to a transparent process. Export the full trail as a timestamped CSV for any application at any time.

Design Note
PBN stepper touch targets need to increase for production
The +/− stepper buttons in the PBN assignment wireframe are currently 28px. This is below the 44px minimum touch target required for mobile (and good practice on desktop). In the production implementation, the stepper buttons should be at minimum 36px for desktop and 44px if a tablet view is ever added. Alternatively, a direct number input (tapping the number to edit it directly) may be a cleaner interaction on smaller viewports.

Open Questions for Your Review

The following questions are consolidated here for your convenience. They represent the areas where your expertise and knowledge of the IOC process will shape the final system. We have noted our working assumption in each case so you can confirm, correct, or redirect.

1
What is the final list of fields on the EoI application form?
We have used the MICO26 form as a starting point. The definitive LA 2028 field list is needed from IOC Media Operations. This is the most time-sensitive question — the sooner we receive this, the easier the implementation. We have a provisional list in place and can build immediately.
2
Are the three accreditation categories correct for EoI — Press, Photographer, ENR?
Based on the MICO26 form. Should this list be different for LA 2028? Are any categories missing, or should any be removed at this stage?
3
What review timeline should we communicate to applicants after they submit?
The confirmation screen currently says "typically 2–4 weeks." We want to reflect the realistic NOC review window from your experience — not a number that causes frustration when the reality differs.
4
Who has the authority to resolve cross-territory duplicate flags — NOC or IOC?
When the same organization applies through multiple NOCs, a "possible duplicate" flag appears on each relevant NOC's dashboard. The flag is shown in the current design but the resolution workflow is not yet built — we need to understand the process before we can design the UI. Can a NOC resolve this independently, or does every cross-territory flag escalate to IOC? The answer determines the flag lifecycle states and whether an escalation workflow is needed.
5
How should IOC be alerted to anomalies — dashboard notification, daily email, or both?
And who on the IOC Media Operations team owns the response process when an anomaly is flagged? We need to understand who acts and how so we can design the right notification workflow.
6
Should unusual volume thresholds vary by NOC territory size?
Currently set at 80 applications per NOC as a uniform threshold. Large press markets (USA, Japan, UK) will naturally attract more applications. Should the alert threshold be higher for these territories, or is a uniform threshold preferable for simplicity?
7
Is Associated Press Australia a separate organization from Associated Press, or a subsidiary?
This question affects how the duplicate detection rules work for large international organizations with offices in multiple countries. Our working assumption is that AP-Australia and AP-US are treated as the same organization applying through different NOCs — a legitimate multi-territory application, not a duplicate. Is this correct?
8
Can NOC teams have more than one administrator on the portal?
The current design supports multiple administrators per NOC. We would like your guidance on whether this is needed for launch — or whether starting with one login per NOC is sufficient, with multi-user support added later.
9
Should known organizations be invited directly, or does everyone self-register?
Our provisional design: organizations already known to the IOC (in Common Codes from prior Games) receive an invitation email with a direct link. New organizations self-register. Please confirm, and advise: who sends the invitations — IOC, or the relevant NOC? And which list of organizations qualifies as "known"?
10
How do we verify that a person registering is genuinely entitled to represent an organization?
This is the most important unresolved design question. If anyone can self-register with any organization name, the system is open to abuse (a random person claiming to represent Reuters). Our working approach — invitation for known orgs, NOC review as the verification gate for unknown orgs — needs your confirmation. We would like to walk through the specific scenarios with IOC OIS before finalizing this design.
11
Does the NOC need a "reserved" quantity in the Press by Number quota assignment?
Some NOCs currently hold back slots informally to cover late changes. Should the portal formalize this as a configurable reserve? If so, does the IOC need to approve or see it?
12
Does the IOC approve NOC quota allocations, or just have visibility?
When a NOC assigns their quota among approved organizations, is the IOC's role to see the result (visibility only), or to formally approve it before it is finalized? The answer determines whether we need an approval workflow in the assignment screen.
13
Once quota allocations are passed from MRP to ACR, is MRP effectively closed — and do any subsequent adjustments happen in ACR?
MRP sets the quota for each approved organization (e.g. Associated Press: 24 credentials) and passes that data to ACR when the Press by Number phase is submitted. What happens after that point is unresolved. Our assumption is that MRP becomes read-only once the allocation is submitted to ACR — but we do not know whether adjustments (a NOC reallocating slots after submission, an IOC-directed change, an organization withdrawing) are expected to flow back through MRP or handled directly in ACR. If adjustments happen in ACR without MRP knowing, the two systems will fall out of sync. If adjustments require changes in MRP first, we need to design a post-submission amendment workflow. This needs to be agreed with the ACR team before we finalize the MRP → ACR integration design.
14
What is the full set of anomaly types the system should detect, and what does resolution look like for each?
The current design detects two conditions: possible duplicate organizations across territories (matched by domain and org name), and unusual application volume from a single NOC (threshold provisionally set at 80, pending IOC guidance). There are likely others — for example, an organization applying under a category that does not match their type, or a contact email domain that does not match the organization's website. We have deliberately not designed the full anomaly detection and resolution system: we do not yet know what anomalies matter, who is responsible for acting on each type, or what "resolved" means in each case. What we do know is that the UI will need to support a lifecycle for each anomaly — raised, under review, resolved, escalated — and that every resolution must be logged to the audit trail. We need IOC OIS input before we can design this.
15
Should AP-US and AP-France be treated as independent organizations, or as the same organization applying through two different territories?
Our working assumption is the flat model: AP-US and AP-France are separate records, each managed by their own NOC, linked only by a shared email domain as a "multi-territory flag." This is how Common Codes and the OCOG accreditation system currently code organizations — different codes, different quotas, different credentials. But we want to confirm this is the right model for MRP before it becomes load-bearing in the dedup logic and org directory. Two follow-on questions: (1) When a different contact person from the New York Times applies for Brisbane 2032, how do they find and claim the NYT record created for LA 2028 — is this a self-service lookup, an IOC-managed invitation, or a NOC-mediated connection? (2) How should MRP handle an organization that changes its name or domain between Games editions?

Feature — v0.2 (May 2026)
Role impersonation — "View As" for IOC super-admins
IOC OIS administrators with super-admin privileges will be able to view the portal exactly as any other user would see it, without logging out or sharing credentials. This is accessed via a "View As" control in the admin header.

Example scenarios:
  • Support a NOC. An IOC administrator sees a complaint from the USOPC that an application is displaying incorrectly. Rather than asking for a screen recording, the IOC admin can select "View As: USOPC Administrator" and see the exact same view the NOC admin is seeing — live data, same filters, same permissions.
  • Verify the applicant experience. Before the portal opens, an IOC admin can select "View As: Applying Organization (AP)" and walk through the entire application flow to confirm it works as expected from the applicant's perspective.
  • Audit a decision. If a media organization disputes a rejection, an IOC admin can view the application detail screen exactly as the NOC reviewer saw it when they made the decision.

Design principles:
  • The "View As" session is read-only. The impersonating admin cannot take actions (approve, reject, submit) on behalf of the impersonated role — they can only observe.
  • A persistent amber banner is shown at the top of every screen while in "View As" mode: "Viewing as: USOPC Administrator — United States. Your actions are not being recorded. Exit View As."
  • All "View As" sessions are logged in the audit trail: who initiated, which role was impersonated, and for how long.
  • The feature is restricted to IOC super-admins only — not available to NOC administrators or standard IOC roles.

This feature significantly reduces the support burden on the IOC OIS team and is essential for pre-launch testing. The role selector already present in the v0.1 prototype header is the design foundation for this — in v0.2 it becomes a formal, audited, permission-gated capability rather than a prototype convenience.
